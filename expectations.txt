Evaluate Alaska Air Group (ALK) earnings using the following framework:

1. Consensus expectations (baseline)

   - Near-term (Q1 2026):
     * EPS ≈ -$1.5 to -$2.0 (loss expected)
     * Expectations have been revised downward
   - Full-year 2026:
     * EPS guidance ≈ $3.50–$6.50 (wide range)
     * Consensus midpoint ≈ $5.2–$5.5
     * Revenue ≈ $15B+

   Interpretation:
   - A weak Q1 is expected and largely priced in.
   - Focus is on forward recovery, not current-quarter results.
   - Treat the quarter as a “reset” period.

2. Priority evaluation factors (ranked)

   2.1 Forward guidance (highest priority)
   - Compare updated full-year and forward-quarter guidance vs prior expectations.
   - Company has already guided below consensus and cut Q1 outlook.
   - If guidance stabilizes or improves → bullish signal.
   - If guidance weakens again → strong bearish signal.
   - Override rule: guidance outweighs Q1 results.

   2.2 Fuel cost outlook
   - Evaluate forward jet fuel assumptions and hedging commentary.
   - Fuel is a major earnings driver (~$0.70 EPS headwind recently).
   - If fuel pressure is framed as temporary → positive.
   - If elevated costs persist → negative.

   2.3 Demand trends (forward-looking)
   - Assess strength of:
     * Corporate travel (currently strong)
     * Leisure travel (mixed: Mexico/Hawaii weaker)
   - Determine whether demand weakness is:
     * Temporary/disruption-driven → neutral/positive
     * Structural/broad-based → negative

   2.4 Pricing power / yields
   - Evaluate ability to:
     * Raise fares
     * Maintain load factors
   - Strong pricing power → supports margins → bullish
   - Weak pricing → margin compression → bearish

   2.5 Cost control and margin trajectory
   - Assess:
     * Non-fuel cost discipline
     * Expected margin recovery in Q2–Q3 (peak season)
   - Lack of margin recovery path → negative signal

3. Expectation vs reality framework

   Bull case:
   - Fuel pressure is temporary
   - Demand strengthens into summer
   - Corporate travel remains strong
   - Guidance stabilizes or improves

   Bear case:
   - Fuel costs remain elevated
   - Demand weakens across regions
   - Guidance is cut again
   - Margin recovery is delayed

4. Priced-in baseline vs surprise scenarios

   Baseline:
   - Weak Q1 loss
   - Some fuel pressure
   - Conservative forward outlook

   Upside surprise:
   - Improved fuel outlook
   - Strong forward bookings / summer demand
   - Guidance raised or tightened upward

   Downside surprise:
   - Additional guidance cuts
   - Broad demand deterioration
   - Worse-than-expected margin pressure

5. Reaction function

   - If:
     * Q1 results are in line
     * AND guidance stabilizes or improves
     * AND demand outlook is constructive
     → classify as BULLISH

   - If:
     * Q1 results are in line or better
     * BUT guidance weakens OR demand deteriorates
     → classify as BEARISH

   - If mixed signals:
     → classify as NEUTRAL with guidance as primary tie-breaker

Output:
- Classification: Bullish / Neutral / Bearish
- Key drivers (2–4 concise points)
- Explicit assessment of:
  * Guidance direction (improving vs deteriorating)
  * Demand quality (temporary vs structural weakness)
  * Margin recovery visibility
